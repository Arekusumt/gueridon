# Research notes — menu engineering & restaurant psychology bibliography

Verification date: 2026-07-10. Every entry in `bibliography.json` was confirmed to exist via
publisher pages, DOIs, official journals (BOE/EUR-Lex) or full-text PDFs. Confidence key:
`verified` = primary source or academic record seen; `secondary` = only solid secondary sources.

## Matrix (menu engineering)

**Kasavana & Smith (1982)** is a *book*, not a journal article — "Menu Engineering: A Practical
Guide to Menu Analysis", Hospitality Publications, Lansing MI (WorldCat OCLC 9550099). It plots
each item on popularity (menu-mix share) vs contribution margin (price minus food cost, in euros,
NOT food-cost %) and prescribes actions per quadrant: Stars (keep/showcase), Plowhorses (raise
margin carefully), Puzzles (promote/reposition/rename), Dogs (drop or hide). The popularity
threshold is conventionally 70% of the expected equal share (1/n x 0.7). A writer should stress
that the matrix uses margin in currency, which biases against cheap items — that is exactly what
**Pavesic (1983)** fixed: his cost/margin analysis crosses food-cost % with the *weighted*
contribution margin (margin x units sold), producing Primes/Standards/Sleepers/Problems. Verified
via ScienceDirect, DOI 10.1016/0278-4319(83)90033-6, International Journal of Hospitality
Management. Pavesic's is the "third approach" after Miller (1980, food-cost %) and Kasavana &
Smith; a fair account mentions all three exist and that academic reviews (e.g. FIU Hospitality
Review) treat none as definitive.

## Pricing & price presentation

**Yang, Kimes & Sessarego (2009)**: real dining-room field study at the CIA's St. Andrew's Cafe.
Three formats: "$20.00", "20." and "twenty dollars". Numeral-only guests spent ~$5.55 (~8%) more
per party. Careful: the popular retelling ("removing the $ sign raises spend") is right, but the
written-out word format did NOT outperform either; the mechanism proposed is pain-of-paying
priming. Published as Cornell Hospitality Report 9(8); a peer-reviewed version appeared in IJHM
(2009) as "Menu price presentation influences on consumer purchase behavior in restaurants".

**Huber, Payne & Puto (1982)**: the decoy/asymmetric-dominance paper (JCR 9(1):90-98). Adding an
option nobody should pick (dominated by the target but not by the competitor) shifts share TOWARD
the target, violating regularity. No single clean percentage exists across its six product
categories — resist quoting one. For menus: a premium anchor dish makes the second-most-expensive
look reasonable; that folk claim is an extrapolation, the study itself is about choice sets.

**Thomas & Morwitz (2005)** (JCR 32(1):54-64): charm pricing works through the LEFT digit —
$2.99 feels meaningfully cheaper than $3.00, but $3.59 vs $3.60 does nothing. So 12,90 vs 13,00
matters; 12,99 vs 12,90 does not (much). **Wadhwa & Zhang (2015)** (JCR 41(5)) adds the luxury
nuance: rounded prices ("24" not "23,87") *feel right* for hedonic, feeling-driven purchases and
increase intent there; precise prices fit utilitarian framing. Combined takeaway for writers:
charm prices for value positioning, clean round prices for premium/experience positioning.

**Omnes rules** — the weak link, kept honest: universally taught in French hospitality schools
(ouverture de gamme <=2.5-3x; middle price tier >= low+high combined; demand/offer ratio 0.9-1.0;
promote near the category average), but there is NO locatable primary publication. Attribution is
inconsistent: most trade sources say "André Omnès"; L'Hôtellerie-Restauration's fiche pratique
credits consultant **Jean Toulemonde** and derives "omnes" from Latin "all" (developed for
cafeteria/retail chains). Cite as trade doctrine ("the so-called Omnes principles"), never as a
peer-reviewed finding. Confidence: secondary.

## Scanning / menu layout

**Yang (2012)** (IJHM 31(3):1021-1029) is the honest counterpoint to the "golden triangle":
infrared eye-tracking showed diners read two-page menus **sequentially, like a book**, slowly and
for information; no gaze concentration at the upper-right "sweet spot". The widely-repeated
golden-triangle/sweet-spot doctrine traces to unpublished/unverifiable industry work (often
attributed to a 1980s Gallup note); no primary source could be verified for it — treat it as
folklore that eye-tracking failed to confirm. **Dayan & Bar-Hillel (2011)** (Judgment and
Decision Making 6(4):333-342) gives layout advice that DID survive testing: within a category
list, items at the beginning or end are chosen up to twice as often as the same items mid-list
(~55/45 split at category level). So: edges of each category > mythical page sweet spots.

## Copy

**Wansink, Painter & van Ittersum (2001)** (Cornell HRA Quarterly 42(6)): the famous +27% is real
and correctly quoted — descriptive labels (geographic/nostalgic/sensory) raised sales of labeled
items 27% in a 6-week field experiment. Caveats for honest use: the venue was a university
cafeteria (N=140), and labels did NOT raise willingness to pay. The 2005 companion (**Food
Quality and Preference 16(5):393-400**) shows labels also improve post-hoc taste/appeal ratings
of the identical dish (7.31 vs 6.83 taste). **Levin & Gaeth (1988)** ("75% lean" vs "25% fat",
JCR 15(3):374-378) is the classic attribute-framing base: state the positive frame; the effect
shrinks but survives actual tasting.

## Tipping

All six classics check out, with one correction: the "thank you on the check" study is **Rind &
Bordia 1995** (JASP 25:745-751), NOT 1996 — the 1996 Rind & Bordia paper is the smiley-face study
(worked for the female server, backfired/neutral for the male). "Thank you" raised tips ~11%
relative (~2 pp, ~16.3->18.1% per secondary sources); adding the server's name in writing added
nothing. **Garrity & Degelman (1990)**: verbal name introduction 23.4% vs 15.0% — but N=42
two-person parties, one server, Sunday brunch; huge effect, small study. **Crusco & Wetzel
(1984)** "Midas Touch" (PSPB 10:512-517): brief hand/shoulder touch raised tips (~12% -> ~14-17%,
rounded secondary figures; exact decimals not verifiable without the paywalled full text);
customers' ratings of the waitress didn't change — nonconscious effect. **Strohmetz et al.
(2002)** candy study: the increase depends on amount AND manner — ~+3.3% relative for one candy,
+14.1% for two, biggest (~+21-23%) when the server gave one, turned away, then "spontaneously"
offered another (perceived personal generosity = reciprocity trigger). **van Baaren et al.
(2003)**: verbal mimicry (literally repeating the order) raised average tips >68% (2.97 vs 1.76
guilders; replication with hypothesis-blind waitress 2.73 vs 1.36) — verified in the primary PDF.
**Lynn**: two solid picks included — Lynn & McCall (2000) meta-analysis (J. Socio-Economics
29(2)): service-tip correlation positive but much weaker than assumed (2,547 parties); and Lynn &
McCall (2009, Cornell Hospitality Quarterly 50(2)): survey of 1,066 servers supporting the
generalizability of tip-enhancing techniques; Lynn's practitioner manual "Mega Tips" estimates
+10-30% from stacking several techniques. Ethical framing note: these are US tipping norms;
effect sizes in Spain (service included, low-tip culture) will differ — the van Baaren study
(Netherlands, tips optional) is the closest cultural analogue.

## Ambience

**Milliman (1986)** (JCR 13(2):286-289): slow-tempo background music stretched dinner from 45 to
56 minutes and raised bar/drink revenue ~40% (~3 extra drinks/table); food spend unchanged.
Secondary sources give $30.47 vs $21.62 bar spend per group — plausible but not verified against
the paywalled original; prefer the duration and ~40% figures. **North, Hargreaves & McKendrick
(1997 Nature brief; 1999 JAP full report)**: French vs German music alternated over a wine
display — 40 French vs 8 German bottles on French-music days; 22 German vs 12 French on
German-music days; only 6 of 44 questioned shoppers admitted any music influence. Supermarket,
not restaurant — say so. **Weight = quality**: two complementary verified studies — Piqueras-
Fiszman, Harrar, Alcaide & Spence (2011, Food Quality & Preference): identical yogurt from a
heavier (hand-held) bowl rated denser, pricier, better; and Magnini & Kim (2016, IJHM 53:42-48):
physically heavier MENUS raise perceived upscaleness and expected service quality (italic font
too; gold paper did nothing). Note: no Piqueras-Fiszman/Spence paper tests menu weight itself —
their line covers plateware/cutlery/bottles; Magnini & Kim is the menu-specific citation.

## Choice

**Iyengar & Lepper (2000)** (JPSP 79(6):995-1006): 24-jam booth drew more browsers, but ~30% of
6-jam tasters bought vs ~3% at 24 jams (31 vs 4 buyers — verified in the paper PDF). BE HONEST:
**Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd (2010)** (JCR 37(3):409-425) meta-analyzed 50 experiments
(N=5,036) and found a mean choice-overload effect of virtually ZERO with huge between-study
variance. Choice overload appears under moderators (unfamiliar/complex options, no dominant
option, no strong prior preferences, time pressure) — conditions that actually DO apply to many
restaurant menus read by first-time visitors, which is the defensible editorial line. Don't quote
"less choice always sells more".

## Law (Spain/Catalonia — for the compliance sidebar)

- **Reglamento (UE) 1169/2011, Anexo II**: the 14 mandatory allergens, verified letter by letter
  in the Official Journal PDF (L 304/43): cereales con gluten; crustáceos; huevos; pescado;
  cacahuetes; soja; leche (incl. lactosa); frutos de cáscara (8 tree nuts listed); apio; mostaza;
  sésamo; dióxido de azufre/sulfitos >10 mg/kg o mg/l; altramuces; moluscos. Applies to
  non-prepacked food in restaurants (Art. 44; in Spain RD 126/2015 — allergen info must be
  available, can be oral if a written record exists on request).
- **Llei 20/1985 (Catalunya), art. 15.2** (consolidated): prohibits alcohol promotions announced
  as "barra libre", "2x1", "3x1" or similar inciting abusive/unlimited consumption, plus any
  advertising of them. Verified in the BOE consolidated text; the clause was ADDED by Ley
  18/2009 (salud pública), d.f. 1.1 — so cite "Llei 20/1985, art. 15.2 (redacción Ley 18/2009)".
  Sanctions run under the health-sanctions regime and can exceed 6,000 EUR for serious offenses.
- **Ley 7/2022, art. 18.3**: hostelería must always offer free non-bottled (tap) water,
  complementary to the paid offer; no direct or indirect charges (glass/ice/service). In force
  since April 2022. Verified against the article text.

## Dropped / could not verify

1. **"Golden triangle" (pro) sources** — the original claim that eyes land mid-page then
   upper-right (often attributed to a Gallup study, ~1987) has no locatable primary source. Only
   the debunking side (Yang 2012) is citable. Do not cite the Gallup triangle as research.
2. **Omnes primary publication** — no book/paper by "André Omnès" found; attribution disputed
   (L'Hôtellerie credits Jean Toulemonde). Kept as `secondary` trade doctrine, not dropped from
   the bibliography, but flagged: never present as an academic study.
3. **Exact decimals for Crusco & Wetzel (12.2/14.4/16.7%)** — repeated in secondary literature
   but not confirmable without the paywalled full text; the JSON uses rounded figures marked as
   secondary-sourced. Study existence and direction: fully verified.
4. **Milliman's dollar figures ($30.47 vs $21.62 bar spend)** — same situation: direction, the
   56/45 minutes and ~+40% drink revenue verified via multiple solid summaries; exact dollar
   amounts left flagged as secondary.
5. **Strohmetz absolute tip percentages (18.95/19.59/21.62/23.0%)** — the paper is verified and
   the relative increases (+3.3%/+14.1%/~+21%) are consistent across sources incl. a document
   citing the paper directly; absolute percentages flagged as secondary.
6. **"Wansink +27%" integrity check** — the figure IS the real one from the 2001 paper (no
   correction needed), but note the cafeteria setting, N=140, and no willingness-to-pay effect.
   Separately: Brian Wansink resigned from Cornell in 2018 after retractions of OTHER papers
   (pizza-buffet/Smarter Lunchrooms lines). The two menu-label papers cited here were not among
   the retracted set as of verification date, but an editorial team should know the author
   carries replication-crisis baggage — consider pairing with Levin & Gaeth 1988 when making
   copy claims.
7. **Rind & Bordia 1996 as "thank you" study** — misattribution in the brief; corrected to 1995
   (1996 = smiley face). Nothing dropped.
